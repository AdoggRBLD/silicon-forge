## Executive Summary

There is no mobile game on the market about FPGA or IC design. The two closest competitors — Shenzhen I/O and Turing Complete — are both PC-only, and both studios have stopped active development. Silicon Forge can own a proven, hungry audience that currently has nowhere to go.

---

## The Direct Competitors

### Shenzhen I/O (Zachtronics, 2016)

- **Platform:** PC only (Steam)
- **Owner estimate:** 200K–500K on Steam
- **Status:** No active development; Zachtronics dissolved in 2024
- **Price:** $14.99 one-time, no IAP, no subscription
- **Reception:** Overwhelmingly Positive
- **Critical metric:** Only ~2% of players completed the main campaign

The 98% completion gap is not a sign that the audience is too small. It's a sign that the difficulty scaffolding is broken. Players want to learn and they want to finish — they fall off because the curve is brutal and there's no structured help.

### Turing Complete (LevelHead, ongoing EA)

- **Platform:** PC only (Steam)
- **Reviews:** 5,027 with Overwhelmingly Positive rating
- **Price:** $19.99, with explicit plans to raise after Early Access
- **Status:** Slow update cadence; small team

Validates willingness to pay $15–20 for a logic-design game. Both competitors lack subscription revenue and lack a free funnel — they can't compound growth.

### Adjacent: Factorio, SpaceChem, Opus Magnum, KSP

These reach the same player psychology (systems thinking, optimization, "I built that") but don't teach real engineering syntax or concepts. They're inspirations, not threats.

## What the Community Is Asking For

Recurring complaints across r/FPGA, r/ECE, r/embedded, and Steam discussion boards:

1. **No bridge between game intuition and real toolchains.** Players who finish Shenzhen I/O still can't open Vivado.
2. **Real toolchains terrify beginners.** Vivado, Quartus, and Yosys all assume an EE degree.
3. **Nothing playable during idle time.** All current tools are desk-bound.

Silicon Forge addresses all three at once: real HDL concepts, mobile-native, gentle onboarding via the trade-game wrapper.

## Strategic Takeaways

**The 98% problem is the business model.** A Drug Wars-style economy where in-game currency unlocks hints, components, and faster iteration converts the frustration that killed Shenzhen I/O into a monetization loop.

**Monetization gap:** Both PC competitors are one-time purchases. A free-to-play mobile game with subscription tiers can capture a 50–100x larger funnel.

**The single existential design decision:** use real HDL concepts and real syntax, not invented puzzle-language. That's what separates a toy from a career accelerator — and it's the position no competitor has taken.

---

## Recommended Positioning

> "The Drug Wars of chip design — playable in 5 minutes, teaches real Verilog, exports a portfolio."

Lead with the trade game. Deliver the engineering education invisibly. Convert engaged players into hardware kit buyers. None of the existing competitors can copy this without rewriting their core engine.
