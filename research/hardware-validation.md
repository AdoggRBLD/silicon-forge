## Executive Summary

The $149 FPGA-trainer-plus-mobile-game bundle has strong precedent on Crowd Supply. Comparable boards have raised $87K–$552K, most exceeding goals by 400–1,500%. The novel element — pairing a physical board with a mobile game — needs a crystal-clear "learning loop" pitch, but the platform actively favors mission-driven educational hardware. This is a fundable product if executed with a working prototype, demo video, open-source toolchain, and a pre-launch community.

## Backer Trust Signals

What every successful Crowd Supply FPGA campaign had:

1. **Working prototype + demo video.** No exceptions.
2. **Open-source hardware files on GitHub.** Universal across all funded campaigns.
3. **Open-source toolchain support.** Yosys / nextpnr / Project IceStorm or equivalent.
4. **Pre-existing community proof.** Discord, GitHub stars, Hackaday writeup, conference workshop, university course.
5. **Mission framing.** Crowd Supply's "for the greater goods" platform leans heavily into educational and open-source positioning.

iCEBreaker validated its educational mission by **using early prototypes to teach workshops at the Hackaday Superconference** before launching the campaign. ULX3S built credibility from its origin as a **digital logic course at the University of Zagreb.** Both are reproducible templates: prove the educational use case publicly *before* you ask for money.

## Time-to-Fund

- **iCEBreaker:** fully funded on day one, within seven hours of launch
- **ULX3S:** exceeded the modest $15K goal within four days
- **Typical campaign duration:** 30–60 days, but well-prepared launches with pre-existing communities hit goal almost immediately and spend the rest of the campaign on stretch goals and word-of-mouth

The lesson: **the campaign isn't where you find your audience. It's where you collect them.** The audience is built in the 6–12 months before launch.

## What This Means for Silicon Forge

### Pre-Launch Checklist (must-haves before campaign goes live)

- [ ] Working prototype board (functional, not pretty)
- [ ] Working game build that talks to the board (the "learning loop" demo)
- [ ] 2–3 minute video showing the loop end-to-end
- [ ] GitHub repo with all hardware files, KiCad sources, and BOM
- [ ] Discord server with 500+ members
- [ ] Hackaday or Hackster project writeup published
- [ ] One workshop or talk delivered (Supercon, hackerspace, university)
- [ ] Open-source toolchain support documented (Yosys/nextpnr or equivalent)

### Pricing Strategy

Stick with $149 for the bundle. Justification:
- Game Bub validated the $250 ceiling for hardware + gaming
- iCEBreaker validated $69–$159 for FPGA hardware
- $149 sits in the proven sweet spot and leaves room for stretch SKUs ($199 Pro, $99 Lite)

### Funding Target

Conservative goal: **$25K–$50K.** Almost certainly hits 4–10× goal if the pre-launch checklist is real. Set the headline goal low so the campaign visibly succeeds early — momentum is a marketing channel.

### Stretch Goals

- $100K — second board variant (Tang Nano vs. iCE40)
- $200K — open-source the entire game engine
- $300K — fund a Tiny Tapeout group buy for top players

## Strategic Takeaways

1. **The $149 bundle is fundable.** Multiple direct precedents, all overfunded.
2. **The mobile-game pairing is the differentiator and the risk.** Lead the pitch with the learning loop, not the silicon.
3. **Build the community before the campaign.** Every successful campaign had this. None skipped it.
4. **Open-source everything.** It's table stakes on this platform; the campaigns that don't, fail.
5. **Aim for day-one funding.** That's the signal a Crowd Supply campaign uses to recruit its second wave of backers.
